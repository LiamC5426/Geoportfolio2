Applied Geospatial Techniques

Applied Geospatial Techniques is a course undertaken during the first year of the Geospatial Science Program. It aims to highlight the three phases of a typical map-making process in sequence, and to develop the required skills required to complete each.

The phases in sequence are:

  • Surveying component
  • GIS component
  • Cartography component

Surveying component
The surveying component involves the practical collection of relevant survey data. It involves use of various surveying tools, such as total stations and optical levels to collect numerous different types of data, from spot heights, to tree locations, to pathways, roads, and terrain variations

GIS component
The GIS component is where the previously collected data is imported into GIS software for manipulation and geoprocessing Actions involved during the GIS component include addition of further contour lines, placement for construction of new physical features, and creation of a color-coded model outlining terrain variation

Cartography component
The cartography component involves the presentation of all data and associate information gathered in a user-friendly format. This mostly involves presentation of data and informartion in a navigational map product, complete with the map itself, formal title, legend and icons, verbal description, and any other information relevant to the user.

Work Experience
The following section outlines my 12-month work experience placement that I undertook in 2022-23

Cardinia Shire Council

Asset Management Role
June 2022 - June 2023
My time at Cardinia Shire Council mostly comprised completion of a "Polygonization Project".

This involved making corrections to the Asset Management Department's "Asset road network", which included correction of polygons representing roads, as well as corresponding polylines representing road centre lines.

Why is this important?
Upgrades and other constructions are constantly occuring accross our roads, from road widening, to duplication, to construction of new roads, to upgrading of intersections, you name it. When this happens, the road network, owned and operated internally by the Council's Asset Management Department, must be manually corrected to adapt to these changes and keep up to the continuously upgrading road system.

In the following screenshot of the project, note that the road polygon (pink) and the road polylines (yellow lines) do not match up exactly with the road on the satellite imagery map. My job was to re-adjust the placements of the polygon and polyline so as to better reflect the road's true geographical position.
